# Phase L.1 — Chapter 1–5 Canvas Route Map

Instructor Note: The prompts below are examples and teaching suggestions. They are not intended as required course assignments. Instructors should decide which prompts, if any, to use, adapt, grade, or replace based on their course goals, syllabus, modality, institutional policies, and student needs.

## Chapter 1: Ethical Perception, Complexity, and the Need for Models

**Purpose:** Introduce students to layered emergence, complexity, and the idea that ethical outcomes may exceed individual intentions.

**Primary public tool:** Capability Lab Schelling emergence simulation — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/capability-lab-public-studio/

**Optional empirical extension:** Light EECSL preview only — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/empirical-ethics-case-simulation-library/

**Minimal route:**
1. Open the Capability Lab.
2. Run the Schelling emergence demonstration.
3. Ask students what the pattern shows that individual intention alone does not explain.
4. Close with the distinction between blame, responsibility, and system architecture.

**Full route:**
1. Run the Traditional Schelling preset.
2. Run an expanded Capability Lab version with repair or shared-space conditions.
3. Compare what changes when the system includes more than local preference.
4. Use the glossary to clarify simulation output versus moral conclusion.

**Suggested public discussion starters:**
- What did the simulation make visible that an ordinary debate about individual choices might hide?
- How can a large-scale pattern emerge even when no single agent intends the whole pattern?
- What would count as a fair intervention in this system?

**Suggested private reflection opportunities:**
- Suggested private exploration: Where have you seen a difference between what people intended and what a system produced?
- Suggested private exploration: How does recognizing emergence change how you assign responsibility?

## Chapter 2: Ethical Attention, PRF, and the Structure of Moral Perception

**Purpose:** Help students see that ethical perception is structured by attention, assumptions, values, language, and interpretive frames.

**Primary public tool:** Glossary + Ethical Lens framing — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/empirical-ethics-case-simulation-library/methods/key-terms-glossary.html

**Optional empirical extension:** Optional Cross-Case Dashboard preview — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/empirical-ethics-case-simulation-library/teaching/cross-case-integration.html

**Minimal route:**
1. Open the Key Terms Glossary.
2. Review PRF-adjacent terms, evidence-governance distinctions, and Teaching FHRI caution language.
3. Ask students to distinguish source facts, model outputs, interpretations, and private applications.

**Full route:**
1. Open the Cross-Case Dashboard.
2. Choose one case card and ask what different observers might notice first.
3. Introduce the public/private boundary: public case analysis is not private confession.
4. Use a short lens audit as a discussion exercise.

**Suggested public discussion starters:**
- What does an ethical lens help us notice that we might otherwise ignore?
- Why should a public case page distinguish evidence, model output, interpretation, and teaching prompt?
- How can two students view the same case differently without either being careless?

**Suggested private reflection opportunities:**
- Suggested private exploration: What kinds of ethical details do you usually notice first?
- Suggested private exploration: What kinds of ethical details might you tend to overlook?

## Chapter 3: Evidence Policy, Warning, and Ethical Lenses

**Purpose:** Show how warning signals can be suppressed, normalized, overwritten, or institutionally ignored under pressure.

**Primary public tool:** Capability Lab Challenger / Boisjoly run mode — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/capability-lab-public-studio/

**Optional empirical extension:** 2008 Financial Crisis — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/empirical-ethics-case-simulation-library/cases/2008-financial-crisis.html

**Minimal route:**
1. Run the Challenger classroom mode.
2. Ask what happened to the warning signal.
3. Use one ethical lens question: whose knowledge could be heard?
4. Close with the difference between private courage and institutional warning capacity.

**Full route:**
1. Run Challenger first.
2. Open the 2008 Financial Crisis EECSL case.
3. Compare engineering warning suppression with financial rating normalization.
4. Apply the evidence-governance distinction: fact, model output, interpretation, hypothesis.

**Suggested public discussion starters:**
- What makes a warning institutionally hearable or dismissible?
- Why is warning not simply a matter of individual courage?
- How does the 2008 case extend the Challenger pattern to a larger institutional system?

**Suggested private reflection opportunities:**
- Suggested private exploration: Have you ever seen a warning ignored in a group or institution? What made it hard to hear?
- Suggested private exploration: What would make it easier for you to speak or listen responsibly under pressure?

## Chapter 4: Systems, Emergence, SAGE, and Responsibility

**Purpose:** Help students understand responsibility in systems where outcomes emerge from feedback, incentives, thresholds, and institutional design.

**Primary public tool:** Schelling + Coordination Under Heat — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/capability-lab-public-studio/

**Optional empirical extension:** Higher Education IPS or 2008 Financial Crisis — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/empirical-ethics-case-simulation-library/teaching/cross-case-integration.html

**Minimal route:**
1. Run a short Schelling demonstration.
2. Run one coordination-under-heat preset.
3. Ask what changed when heat, voice, trust, or repair changed.
4. Connect the result to SAGE: seeing, analyzing, generating, engaging.

**Full route:**
1. Run Schelling Traditional versus Emergent Complexity view.
2. Run Coordination Under Heat using a stress or repair preset.
3. Open the Cross-Case Dashboard.
4. Ask students to identify one T1 repair and one T2 repair possibility.

**Suggested public discussion starters:**
- When does responsibility belong partly to system architecture?
- What changes when coordination is technically successful but ethically unhealthy?
- How does SAGE help avoid jumping from first impression to final judgment?

**Suggested private reflection opportunities:**
- Suggested private exploration: Where in your life do systems make good action easier or harder?
- Suggested private exploration: How might SAGE help you slow down before judging a complex situation?

## Chapter 5: Social Contract, Justice, and the Moral Commons

**Purpose:** Connect Rawls, Nussbaum, John Hope Franklin, functional equivalence, Maathai-style shared task coordination, and the moral commons.

**Primary public tool:** Civic coordination route with Repair preset — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/capability-lab-public-studio/

**Optional empirical extension:** GEO Florida + Higher Education IPS — https://rmertzman-tech.github.io/empirical-ethics-case-simulation-library/cases/geo-florida-prison.html

**Minimal route:**
1. Introduce formal rights versus actual capabilities.
2. Use the civic coordination Repair preset.
3. Ask how different PRFs can support the same shared task for different authentic reasons.
4. Connect to John Hope Franklin as the emotional anchor.

**Full route:**
1. Run civic coordination with Repair preset.
2. Open GEO Florida as the empirical justice case.
3. Use Higher Education IPS as a rights-to-capabilities extension.
4. Ask students to distinguish Rawlsian fairness, Nussbaum capabilities, and functional equivalence.

**Suggested public discussion starters:**
- What is the difference between having a formal right and having the actual capability to use it?
- How can different communities cooperate without sharing the same worldview?
- What does the moral commons require if justice is more than formal permission?

**Suggested private reflection opportunities:**
- Suggested private exploration: Where have you seen a gap between formal opportunity and actual capability?
- Suggested private exploration: What shared task could different communities support for different authentic reasons?
