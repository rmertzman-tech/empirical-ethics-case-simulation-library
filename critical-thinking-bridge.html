Why this bridge matters

Students often experience ethical situations with immediate felt certainty. Something seems unfair, dangerous, manipulative, admirable, cruel, or dignifying. Public simulations then show that ethical reality is also uncertain, modeled, system-shaped, and open to competing interpretations.

The Critical Thinking Bridge helps students preserve both sides of agency: the seriousness of phenomenal moral experience and the discipline of public, revisable reasoning.

Prompt governance:

The prompts and questions on this page are examples, suggestions, opportunities, or explorations. They are not automatic graded assignments. Instructors decide which prompts, if any, students should use, adapt, submit, discuss, or ignore.

Privacy boundary:

Do not enter private personal, family, medical, legal, employment, or financial information into public tools. Public tools are for shared case analysis. Personal application belongs only in private course spaces when your instructor directs it.

OS1 → OS2 bridge flow

1. OS1 felt response

What feels morally obvious, troubling, admirable, unfair, coercive, or urgent?

2. Claim

What public claim does that response suggest?

3. Grounds

What public evidence, simulation result, or case detail supports the claim?

4. Warrant

Why do those grounds support the claim? What value, theory, or principle connects them?

5. Qualifier

How certain is the claim? What limits or uncertainties should be acknowledged?

6. Rebuttal

What could challenge, weaken, or complicate the claim?

7. OS2 coordination

What shared action, repair proposal, or public question could others examine?

Higher Education IPS

OS1 starting point: This institution seems fair or unfair; something about opportunity, debt, support, or extraction feels morally important.

Bridge claim: A public-facing institution can satisfy formal access requirements while still failing to provide agency-supporting capabilities.

The 2008 Financial Crisis

OS1 starting point: The crisis feels like greed, betrayal, deception, or irresponsibility.

Bridge claim: The crisis involved institutionalized risk transfer and evidence-policy failure, not merely individual moral weakness.

GEO Group Florida

OS1 starting point: The case may feel wrong because confinement, profit, vulnerability, and public responsibility are entangled.

Bridge claim: A prison-adjacent public-private institution must be evaluated by how it handles captive vulnerability, not only by contract performance.

Florida Nursing Homes and the Visible Parasite

OS1 starting point: The case may feel disturbing because care, vulnerability, ownership opacity, and profit are combined.

Bridge claim: Care institutions can become ethically parasitic when extraction is hidden behind formal service delivery and vulnerable persons cannot see or contest the ledger.
