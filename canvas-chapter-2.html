Student privacy rule:

Do not enter private personal, family, medical, legal, employment, or other sensitive information into public case pages. Public tools are for shared classroom analysis. Personal application belongs in a private course notebook, journal, or Navigator route when your instructor directs you there.

Minimal teaching route

  1. Open the Key Terms Glossary.
  2. Review PRF-adjacent terms, evidence-governance distinctions, and Teaching FHRI caution language.
  3. Ask students to distinguish source facts, model outputs, interpretations, and private applications.

Full teaching route

  1. Open the Cross-Case Dashboard.
  2. Choose one case card and ask what different observers might notice first.
  3. Introduce the public/private boundary: public case analysis is not private confession.
  4. Use a short lens audit as a discussion exercise.

Suggested public discussion starters

Note: Your instructor will tell you which questions, if any, you should answer for class. The prompts below are examples for reflection and discussion.

  • What does an ethical lens help us notice that we might otherwise ignore?
  • Why should a public case page distinguish evidence, model output, interpretation, and teaching prompt?
  • How can two students view the same case differently without either being careless?

Instructor run notes

  • Keep student PRF work private unless students freely choose to share.
  • Use Chapter 2 to stabilize vocabulary before later simulations become more complex.
  • Avoid turning private prompts into required disclosure.

Glossary caution block

Teaching FHRI reminder:

Teaching FHRI is a classroom simulation proxy. It helps compare model configurations and ask better ethical questions about agency, resilience, repair, and institutional pressure. It is not a validated public audit score unless a separate empirical methodology, weighting model, calibration process, and verification procedure are supplied.
