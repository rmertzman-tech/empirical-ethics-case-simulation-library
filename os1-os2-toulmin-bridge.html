Phenomenology first; formalism second

The bridge begins with moral experience, not with abstraction. But it does not stop with experience. It asks students to translate felt moral certitude into a claim that can be publicly examined through evidence, warrants, qualifications, and rebuttals.

Conceptual distinction

OS1

First-person, PRF-shaped moral perception: what seems obvious, threatening, unfair, sacred, admirable, or urgent.

Meta-conscious step

The reflective pause where the student asks: what claim am I making, and what assumptions connect my evidence to my conclusion?

OS2

Shared public reasoning: claims, grounds, warrants, qualifiers, rebuttals, and repair proposals others can evaluate.

Toulmin structure

  • Claim: What should be believed or done?
  • Grounds: What evidence supports it?
  • Warrant: Why do these grounds support the claim?
  • Backing: What theory, value, precedent, or principle supports the warrant?
  • Qualifier: How certain is the claim, and under what limits?
  • Rebuttal: What could weaken or complicate the claim?
  • Repair / coordination statement: What shared action or testable next step follows?

Relation to simulation

Simulation results should be used as grounds or prompts, not as automatic conclusions. The student must still articulate the claim, warrant, qualifier, and rebuttal.
