Core public question: How can a care institution formally organized around resident protection become structurally capable of shifting risk, cost, and vulnerability onto residents, workers, families, and public systems?

Teaching purpose: Students learn to distinguish agent-level effort from structural ceilings, identify when accurate measurement becomes a weak T2 intervention, and design T1 repairs that make hidden extraction architecture visible and enforceable.

Responsibility caution:

This case should not blame nurses, aides, families, residents, or individual regulators. It examines ownership, capital, staffing, incentive, and information structures that can absorb care capacity before it reaches vulnerable people.

SAGE Scenario: Maria and Mrs. Rodriguez

Scenario:

Maria, RN, works at a PE-acquired nursing home in Florida. She has 14 residents on her shift. The facility’s FHRI_max is very low. Mrs. Rodriguez needs about 30 more minutes of mobility work to prevent a pressure ulcer. Maria has about 8 minutes. She has filed incident reports about staffing ratios. They were logged but not acted on. Tonight she finds a pressure ulcer beginning on Mrs. Rodriguez’s heel.

T1/T2 Intervention Classifier

Understanding which type an action is determines whether it can close the coordination gap.

Type 1 — Structural

Changes ε, φ, ρ, σ, or μ directly. Raises FHRI_max. Durable because structural coordination capacity actually changes.

Type 2 — Agent / Process

Improves agents, incentives, or processes inside unchanged structural parameters. Valuable, but cannot exceed FHRI_max.
