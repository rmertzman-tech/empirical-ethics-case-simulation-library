Minimal teaching route

  1. Open the Capability Lab.
  2. Run the Schelling emergence demonstration.
  3. Ask students what the pattern shows that individual intention alone does not explain.
  4. Close with the distinction between blame, responsibility, and system architecture.

Full teaching route

  1. Run the Traditional Schelling preset.
  2. Run an expanded Capability Lab version with repair or shared-space conditions.
  3. Compare what changes when the system includes more than local preference.
  4. Use the glossary to clarify simulation output versus moral conclusion.

Suggested public discussion starters

Note: Your instructor will tell you which questions, if any, you should answer for class. The prompts below are examples for reflection and discussion.

  • What did the simulation make visible that an ordinary debate about individual choices might hide?
  • How can a large-scale pattern emerge even when no single agent intends the whole pattern?
  • What would count as a fair intervention in this system?

Suggested private reflection opportunities

Instructor Note: The prompts below are examples and teaching suggestions. They are not intended as required course assignments. Instructors should decide which prompts, if any, to use, adapt, grade, or replace based on their course goals, syllabus, modality, institutional policies, and student needs.

  • Suggested private exploration: Where have you seen a difference between what people intended and what a system produced?
  • Suggested private exploration: How does recognizing emergence change how you assign responsibility?

Instructor run notes

  • Do not overload Chapter 1 with every EECSL case.
  • Use Schelling as the key doorway into complexity, not as a full theory of social life.
  • Emphasize that simulation results invite inquiry; they do not automatically settle ethical judgment.

Glossary caution block

Teaching FHRI reminder:

Teaching FHRI is a classroom simulation proxy. It helps compare model configurations and ask better ethical questions about agency, resilience, repair, and institutional pressure. It is not a validated public audit score unless a separate empirical methodology, weighting model, calibration process, and verification procedure are supplied.
